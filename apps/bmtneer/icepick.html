Information scientists and, increasingly, humanists studying literature and history are using statistical methods to detect meaningful patterns across large bodies of text through a technique called topic modeling. Although digitized newspapers and periodicals have proven to be popular corpora for topic modeling, the method’s usefulness is limited by the granularity of the source documents: and in the case of mass-digitized historical newspapers and magazines, entire issues, or even entire volumes, become single documents. Blue Mountain’s structured metadata makes it simple to “unravel” the snaking columns of text into discrete articles for topic analysis, like the common analyses performed on the itemized digitized corpora of scientific journals.

The aggregation and clustering of content zones encoded in METS/ALTO make it possible for topic modeling algorithms to perform much more fine-grained analysis of magazines and newspapers than they can perform using unzoned, “dirty-OCR’ed” texts. Using Blue Mountain Icepick, researchers will be able to study and compare the abstract topics occurring in the works of two authors, for example, or to compare the topics in a magazine’s articles with those in its advertising.
